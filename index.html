Section 01

The Gap: Why Parkinson's Patients Are Underserved

Parkinson's disease is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder in the U.S. — yet resource access, especially in rural and underserved communities, remains deeply fragmented.

I sat in on focus groups last semester and at the beginning of this semester, where I listened to patients and caregivers describe their experiences navigating Parkinson's care. I transcribed those conversations — which were later coded and used as data. What became clear was that this wasn't just a lack of resources. It was a system that people were actively struggling to navigate, often alone, and often without anyone showing them where to start.
🏥
Fragmented Care Systems
Patients navigate neurology, PT, OT, speech therapy, social work, and nutrition independently — with little coordination or shared resource guidance.
📍
Rural Resource Gaps
Southern Illinois lacks consolidated, region-specific PD resources. Patients in Carbondale and surrounding areas must independently piece together care options.
📚
Health Literacy Barriers
Medical information is often written at a graduate reading level. Patients and caregivers struggle to translate clinical language into actionable daily decisions.
👥
Caregiver Burden
Family caregivers — often untrained — bear the weight of care coordination without structured support, education, or resource maps.
Section 02

Project Overview

A collaborative, community-responsive effort to design a practical, regionally-specific Parkinson's disease resource toolkit — built on what patients and caregivers actually told us, not assumptions about what they needed.

Phase 1 · Research
Focus Groups, Needs Assessment & Literature Review
The toolkit was not developed in isolation — it was grounded in coded focus group data gathered directly from patients and caregivers. Those conversations identified real barriers, real gaps, and real needs. That data shaped every section that followed.
Phase 2 · Design
Section-by-Section Toolkit Development
7 sections assigned to team members. Winston led Section 4: Medical Professionals to Contact — regional provider directories, certified clinician finders, and specialist resources.
Phase 3 · Outreach
Provider Verification & Community Engagement
Contacted regional nutrition providers (Branz Nutrition Counseling confirmed inclusion), verified DME suppliers, and mapped local support systems.
Phase 4 · Integration
Team Synthesis & Formatting
Sections integrated into unified toolkit with consistent design, citations, and regional specificity across Southern and Central Illinois.
Ongoing
Review, Revision, and Dissemination Planning
Working with Dr. Jurkowski to finalize deliverables for community distribution and potential publication use.
Section 03

Toolkit Design: Seven Domains of Care

The toolkit is organized as an integrated system — each section stands alone but connects to the whole.

🏠
Section 1 · Home Adaptations
Mobility aids, fall prevention, bathroom safety, adaptive dining equipment, bed mobility, and communication technology.
🥦
Section 2 · Nutrition
Brain-healthy eating, protein/medication balance, symptom management through diet, and daily nutrition checklists.
🏃
Section 3 · Exercise
Evidence-based exercise types (PWR!Moves, LSVT BIG), regional exercise resources, and motor benefit research.
🩺
Section 4 · Medical Professionals
Winston's section. Regional provider directory: physicians, PT/OT/SLP, movement disorder specialists, LSVT and PWR!Moves certified clinicians.
🤝
Section 5 · Social Support
Regional PD support groups, Area Agencies on Aging, caregiver resources, and community connection programs.
💊
Section 6 · Medications
Medication questions to ask your doctor, "on/off" period management, fall risks, food interactions, and timing guidance.
🌐
Section 7 · Online & Other Resources
Key organizations (APDA, Michael J. Fox Foundation, LSVT Global), useful apps, YouTube channels, and podcasts for patients and caregivers.

Section 05

Theoretical Framework

The toolkit is grounded in four interconnected social work theories — each one shaping how the resource was designed and for whom.

🔗
Systems Theory
PD does not exist in isolation. The patient, family, healthcare system, community, and environment form an interconnected whole. The toolkit mirrors this — each section connects to the others, reflecting how changes in one domain ripple across all others.
🌍
Person-in-Environment (PIE)
Understanding PD requires seeing the person in their full environmental context — rural geography, income, housing, family structure. Section 4's regional provider directory was built with this lens: if you're in Marion, IL, your resources look different than St. Louis.
💪
Empowerment Theory
Patients and caregivers should be active agents in their own care — not passive recipients. The toolkit is designed to build knowledge, build confidence, and build capacity: clear language, actionable steps, and real local options they can actually call.
📖
Health Literacy Framework
Medical information must be accessible. The toolkit intentionally uses plain language, plain-English explanations of complex concepts (on/off periods, freezing of gait, levodopa timing), and visual aids — meeting people where they are.

Section 07

Challenges & Barriers

Real research work surfaces real obstacles. These challenges shaped the toolkit's design as much as its successes did.

🗺
Geographic Scarcity
Southern Illinois is medically underserved. Identifying verified, active PD specialists within a reasonable distance of Carbondale required extensive cross-referencing of national directories with local availability.
📞
Provider Verification
Online databases and provider lists are often outdated. Confirming that listed professionals actually see PD patients, accept new patients, and are still practicing required direct outreach.
📏
Scope & Depth Balance
A toolkit must be comprehensive enough to be useful — but simple enough to be used. Finding the right depth across seven distinct domains while maintaining accessible language was an ongoing tension.
🔄
Information Currency
PD treatment guidelines, medications, and approved therapies evolve. Designing a toolkit that is accurate now while acknowledging it will require periodic updates required intentional framing throughout.
Section 08

Cultural & Ethical Considerations

A toolkit that doesn't account for who is actually receiving it will fail the people who need it most.

🗣
Language Accessibility
All toolkit content was written at a general adult reading level. Complex clinical terms are explained in plain language immediately upon use — not assumed, not glossed over.
💰
Economic Realities
Resources are tiered by cost. Free and low-cost options are identified alongside standard options. Equipment reuse programs (Paraquad, IATP) were specifically included to address economic barriers.
📍
Geographic Equity
Resources are mapped to specific regions — St. Louis, Edwardsville, Springfield, Carbondale — so that rural patients have access to what is actually available to them, not just what exists somewhere.
🔒
Patient Autonomy
The toolkit informs and empowers — it does not prescribe. Medical decisions are framed as conversations between patients and their providers, not directives from a document.
🤲
Caregiver Recognition
Caregivers are explicitly included as intended users throughout the toolkit — their burden, their questions, and their needs are addressed directly, not as an afterthought.
📋
Informed Consent
All outreach to providers (nutrition, DME, clinical specialists) was transparent about the toolkit's purpose and required affirmative confirmation before any provider was listed.

Section 12

Self-Reflection

What this project taught me — about research, about Parkinson's disease, about social work, and about myself as a practitioner.

On the Work Itself
Before this project, I knew what Parkinson's disease was — but I didn't understand what it felt like to live with it. Sitting in on focus groups changed that. I wasn't reading about barriers — I was transcribing them, in people's own words. When you hear a caregiver describe what it takes just to get their person to the right doctor, the system's failure stops being abstract.
On Research as Practice
I came into this project thinking of research and practice as separate tracks. I left understanding they're the same thing, done at different scales. Transcribing focus groups, coding themes, verifying providers, calling a nutrition practice to ask if they'd be listed — that's all practice. This project showed me how research becomes intervention.
On My Path Forward
This project reinforced why I'm heading into the Advanced Standing MSW at UIUC. I want to do research that touches real people. This toolkit is something I can point to and say: someone's life might be easier because of this work. That's the kind of social worker I'm becoming — and the kind of practice I intend to build.
